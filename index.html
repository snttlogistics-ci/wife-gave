Johann Rupert and his wife gave land titles in South Africa

  • Francis Lathrop
  • All Categories
  • 0 Comment

In South Africa, the granting of property rights to poor people is particularly difficult because the homes they live in are trapped in complicated land registration systems. 

South African billionaire Johann Rupert and his wife, Gaynor, worked hard with Stellenbosch’s local officials and the Free Market Foundation to highlight the legalities of poor property ownership. Hundreds have been turned over to the land registry, more to come.

South African billionaire Johann Rupert and his wife, Gaynor, worked hard with Stellenbosch’s local officials and the Free Market Foundation to highlight the legalities of poor property ownership. Hundreds have been turned over to the land registry, more to come.

Dr. Johann Rupert and his wife Gaynor gave Kaya Mandi, Klapmuts, Kylemore, Franschhoek, and Le Roux 132 full title deeds. This was done by Ruperts ‘ goodwill and the efforts of Stellenbosch’s Mayor Cllr Gesie van Deventer and her team working with the Free Market Foundation (FMF) Khaya Lam (my home) on land reform at Stellenb’s historic town hall.

The ceremony was also attended by Master of Ceremonies Tabiso Mfeya (Head of Planning and Economic Development at Stellenbosch), Leon Louw (Executive Director FMF), and Temba Nolutshungu (President of Cape Town at FMF).

For more than 40 years, the FMF has championed the cause of turning the various forms of apartheid titles found in townships into absolute, unmistakable property of current owners. Currently, Ruperts has funded 1,000 titles in Stellenbosch and 1,000 in Graff Reinet, where Dr. Rupert’s father Anton was born. To date, 585 title documents have been issued in Stellenbosch, there will be more to come.

In collaboration with Khaya Lam and the Municipality, Johann and Gaynor Rupert are bringing a meaningful difference to the lives of ordinary South Africans and, in particular, the poorest people living in Stellenbosch. The common objective is to bring about a genuine economic and social transformation through property ownership. Once occupants enter the building, they enter the hall as tenants. When they leave, they are homeowners in possession of a fully tradable freeholder tenure, taking their first step toward more real economic empowerment.

In his speech before the title deed ceremony, Johann Rupert claimed that his family was incredibly lucky in the wealth they had accumulated and needed to make use of it for the greater good.

“It is easy to give away money but more difficult to find organizations that spend the time to use the funds properly and efficiently,” he said and commended the FMF and Khaya Lam for their ability to do this.
